# The problem and the buyer

## Who hurts

Compliance Ops operators at mid-market cosmetic CMOs. In our primary persona she's Sarah — five years in the industry, lives in Outlook and SharePoint, owns 80–200 suppliers across two facilities. Her day starts with 40+ unread emails from suppliers, renewals she's chasing, retailer auditors asking for evidence, and a brand QA team asking why the allergen letter for ingredient X is 14 months old.

Her operational reality: the evidence she needs is scattered across six systems that don't talk. When a retailer calls an audit, she builds the response by hand. When a supplier's certificate expires, she finds out from the brand's QA team, not her own system. When she gets hit by a bus, the institutional knowledge walks out with her.

## Who pays

The CEO. Jim, in our personas. He doesn't see Sarah's inbox, but he sees the output of her pain: retailer chargebacks ($50–250k per incident, often multiple per year), audit-prep overtime (1–3 FTE-weeks per audit, 4–8 audits per year), and bids lost because the CMO couldn't produce supplier evidence fast enough to make the retailer's cutoff.

A 2-facility mid-market CMO absorbs 3–7% of annual revenue in compliance-adjacent waste. On $50M of revenue, that's $1.5–3.5M a year. Our seat of $30–80k ARR is a rounding error against that.

## Who champions

Gary, the Procurement Lead. He's not the user; he has an episodic relationship with the product. But he's the one who decides whether Sarah gets software at all. His pain is onboarding — qualifying a new supplier is a 90-day document-chase today and he'd kill to compress it.

Rachel, the Quality Manager, is adjacent. She cares about ingredient-level evidence trails for change control. We serve her partially; she's a Year-2 user as the product deepens into QMS-adjacent surface.

## Why software doesn't already solve this

The existing options are either too heavy or too light.

**Too heavy:** Veeva Vault, MasterControl, Arena, ComplianceQuest. Enterprise QMS vendors with 6–18 month implementations, $200k+ license fees, IT-project install. Mid-market CMOs can't justify the cost, can't spare the IT resources, and don't need 80% of the feature surface. These tools target Johnson & Johnson, not Elevation Labs.

**Too light:** SharePoint folder structure, Excel trackers, Smartsheet, the supplier portal module on whatever ERP they bought in 2015. Every CMO has tried this. It breaks the moment you have more than 40 suppliers. Every operator has the same story: "we had a system for a while, but Jane left and now no one updates it."

**Adjacent but wrong shape:** Vanta, Drata, Secureframe sell continuous compliance — for SaaS security. Their sales motion (self-serve SMB) and buyer (IT/Security) don't map to regulated physical manufacturing. SAP Ariba, GEP, Coupa sell procurement master data — no evidence layer. Consulting firms (PwC, Deloitte compliance practices) do custom work at $500/hour and leave no product behind.

The gap is specific: a daily operator workflow for supplier evidence in regulated physical supply chains, priced for mid-market, AI-native so it actually handles the mess of emails and PDFs without requiring clean data upfront.

## The economic case in one paragraph

A Compliance Ops operator costs a CMO $75–95k loaded. She spends roughly half her time on evidence reconciliation. Valent recovers 60% of that time immediately (from week one) and the rest over six months as the evidence graph fills out. That's $25–50k of recovered operator capacity. Layer on one avoided retailer chargeback per year ($50–250k), one preserved bid per year ($200k–$2M of preserved revenue), and the ROI math closes before you touch the expansion surface. The seat pays for itself in month three.
