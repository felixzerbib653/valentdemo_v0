# Competitive landscape

The honest version. Every competitor has a real strength. Our positioning is the gap that none of them fill.

## Vanta, Drata, Secureframe — the analogy, not the competitor

These companies built continuous compliance as a category. The sales motion is brilliant. The product is excellent. The buyer is IT/Security at a SaaS company. They will not enter our market in the next 24 months because their evidence plane (API pulls from cloud infra) doesn't translate to physical supply chains, their buyer (CISO) doesn't exist at a CMO, and their pricing model (self-serve to mid-market SaaS) doesn't fit white-glove onboarding for regulated manufacturing.

What we steal from them: the Vanta analogy in the elevator pitch, the artifact-driven distribution model, the "operator workflow not IT project" positioning, the price band intuition.

Risk: if Vanta acquires a vertical entry point in cosmetics or food in 2026–2027, the analogy becomes a competitive frame. We'd rather be acquired into that thesis than compete with it.

## Veeva Vault, MasterControl, Arena, ComplianceQuest — enterprise QMS

The enterprise quality management vendors. They sell to brands, large CMOs, pharma, medical device. Strengths: deep regulatory expertise, validated environments, FDA familiarity. Weaknesses: 6–18 month implementations, $200k+ license entry, IT-project deployment, feature surface designed for QA Director not Compliance Ops. They will not move down-market in the next 24 months because their cost structure can't support mid-market pricing and their channel partners optimize for large deals.

Our wedge is below their floor. By the time they'd need to enter mid-market, we have the evidence graph and the operator habit.

Risk: Veeva launches a "Vault Lite" in 2027. Plausible but late. We've established by then.

## SAP Ariba, Coupa, GEP, Jaggaer — procurement platforms

Procurement and source-to-pay vendors. They have supplier master data and contract management, no evidence layer. They serve a different buyer (CPO, not Compliance Ops). They will not enter compliance workflow because their product surface is procurement transaction management and bolting evidence on is a non-trivial pivot.

We will integrate with them as a data source — supplier master data is useful — but they don't compete for our seat.

## Sphera, Enablon, Intelex — EHS/sustainability

Environmental health & safety platforms. Some compliance overlap (chemical inventories, REACH, OSHA). They serve large industrial manufacturers. The buyer is the EHS director, not Compliance Ops at a cosmetic CMO. The product surface is heavier, the pricing higher, the regulatory focus different.

Adjacent but not direct.

## TraceGains, FoodLogiQ, FoodReady — food and supplement adjacencies

This is the closest direct competitor surface, in food and supplements rather than cosmetics. TraceGains in particular has a supplier compliance product for food manufacturers. Strengths: domain knowledge in their vertical, established brand, food-industry relationships. Weaknesses: pre-AI architecture, no operator-grade UX, no MoCRA cosmetic surface, no expansion into the broader trust graph thesis.

If we expand into food (Year 4), this is the competitor we run at. Until then, they're a useful proof point that supplier compliance software works as a category.

## QAD, Plex, Infor — manufacturing ERP supplier modules

ERP vendors with supplier-management bolt-ons. Generally weak as standalone compliance products, generally embedded in customer relationships that are hard to displace. We don't compete with the ERP itself — we live alongside it and extract supplier evidence as a separate workflow.

## In-house tools and SharePoint — the real competitor

This is the actual incumbent. Every CMO has built some version of "supplier folder structure plus an Excel tracker plus a shared Outlook inbox plus a SharePoint list of FEI numbers." The pain we displace is not Veeva — it's the in-house duct tape.

What this means for sales: every demo cycle starts with "show us how you do this today" and the customer realizes the gap within five minutes. The competitor is inertia, not a vendor.

## Consultants — PwC, Deloitte, BSI compliance practices

Big-four compliance consulting practices serve the brand side, not the CMO side. They run audits, they don't run software. They are not in our market and don't intend to be. We may eventually partner with them as a delivery channel into the brand-side enterprise market, but that's a Series B conversation.

## How to position in a competitive slide

Three quadrants. X-axis: enterprise (left) ↔ mid-market (right). Y-axis: heavy QMS (top) ↔ operator workflow (bottom).

Top-left: Veeva, MasterControl, Arena (enterprise heavy). Top-right: TraceGains-adjacent (mid-market QMS-leaning). Bottom-left: empty (no one builds operator workflow at enterprise scale). Bottom-right: us, alone, with Vanta as the analogy in the cell next door.

That visual works because the gap is real. Don't over-clutter the chart with logos that don't actually compete — VCs see through it. Three to four logos per quadrant, our cell empty except for us.
