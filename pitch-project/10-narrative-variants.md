# Narrative variants

Three pitch narratives for three investor theses. Same underlying facts, different emphasis. Pick based on the fund's stated thesis and partner background.

The core four beats — wedge, buyer, artifact, expansion — appear in all three. What shifts is the framing slide and the closing vision.

## Variant A — Vertical SaaS

**Best for:** Bessemer, Battery, Insight (at seed), ICONIQ, Bain Capital Ventures, Craft. Any fund with a strong vertical-software thesis or a partner who's backed Procore, Toast, ServiceTitan, Vanta.

**Opening frame:** "Vanta for supplier compliance in regulated physical manufacturing, starting with cosmetic CMOs under MoCRA."

**Slide sequence:** operator pain → regulatory catalyst → product demo → artifact moment → ACV and sales motion → expansion by vertical → team and ask.

**The hook:** Vanta's category playbook applied to an adjacent vertical where the incumbents are 20 years old and the buyer is underserved. Mid-market, SaaS economics, compounding evidence graph.

**Closing vision:** "We're building the trust infrastructure for regulated physical supply chains. Cosmetics first, supplements and food next. The SaaS category that replaces three generations of QMS."

**Watch out for:** the vertical SaaS partner will want to see ACV, sales cycle, and logo benchmark data. Have the math ready: $30–80k ACV, 3–6 month cycles, operator-driven deals. If the partner pushes on expansion across verticals, the answer is "same product shape, modular pillar data."

## Variant B — Wedge-to-platform / Data infrastructure

**Best for:** Benchmark, Sequoia, Greylock, Accel, Index (at seed). Any fund that invests in workflow products that become data platforms — Segment, dbt, Mercury, Stripe, Plaid.

**Opening frame:** "Compliance is the wedge; the real product is the evidence graph for regulated supply chains."

**Slide sequence:** regulatory and buyer pain → wedge product → the evidence graph as the spine → Year 2 bidding intelligence → Year 3 margin and substitution → vertical portability of the graph → team and ask.

**The hook:** Under the workflow is a data asset — continuously updated, structured, per-supplier trust data — that becomes the primary source of truth for commercial decisions in regulated manufacturing. Bidding, margin, procurement optimization all flow from the same spine. This is Segment-for-compliance-evidence as a platform thesis.

**Closing vision:** "The evidence graph is infrastructure. Whoever owns it owns the downstream commerce data. We win the graph in cosmetics, extend it to adjacent verticals, and become the trust layer for how regulated physical products get sourced and sold."

**Watch out for:** platform-thesis partners can talk themselves out of a seed deal by demanding proof of expansion in the current product. The discipline is to gate the expansion clearly: we're building the wedge now, the graph's portability is proven in the data model, the expansion slide is the upside case not the base case.

## Variant C — Regulated supply chain / Operational AI

**Best for:** Founders Fund, Lux, 8VC, Khosla, NFX. Any fund that invests in industrial and supply chain infrastructure, or in vertical operational AI (applying LLMs to messy industry workflows).

**Opening frame:** "Regulatory compliance is compounding across physical supply chains. We're the AI-native layer that makes unstructured supplier evidence legible."

**Slide sequence:** regulatory surface compounding (MoCRA + FSMA + DSCSA + REACH + retailer overlays) → AI unlock in 2023 → why incumbents can't catch up → the evidence graph → expansion across regulated physical categories → team and ask.

**The hook:** The trillion-dollar regulated physical economy — cosmetics, food, supplements, pharma, specialty chemicals — is running compliance on spreadsheets because the evidence plane was too unstructured for pre-LLM software. That just changed. We're the AI-native category entrant with a real wedge into cosmetics and a proven product that already handles the mess.

**Closing vision:** "Physical supply chains are becoming more regulated, not less. The operational AI layer for compliance — ingest, extract, score, bundle — is a durable category. We're the first credible entrant in it."

**Watch out for:** supply-chain partners have specific category expectations. Expect questions on ERP integrations, certificate-of-analysis handling, lot-traceability. The answer is we're upstream of those — supplier-level evidence, not lot-level traceability — and that's intentional because it lets us serve mid-market where lot-level adds too much implementation cost.

## How to pick

Most funds map cleanly to one of the three. If a fund partner came from vertical SaaS (Vanta, Procore, Toast), lead with Variant A. If they came from data/platform companies (Segment, Stripe, dbt), Variant B. If they're supply-chain or deep-industrial (Flexport, Hadrian, Anduril-adjacent operational tech), Variant C.

When in doubt, Variant A is the safest. It's the narrative the demo was built for and the artifact story is strongest.

## What stays constant

No matter the variant:

1. The demo leads with the Trust Grid, not a slide. The product is the asset.
2. The audit bundle is demonstrated live, not described.
3. The expansion slide is one slide, late in the deck, and gated behind a "we're selling Year 1 today" framing.
4. MoCRA is the wedge; the evidence graph is the spine; bidding and margin are the expansion — never mixed into the wedge.
5. The CEO audience (Jim archetype) should be able to repeat "wedge, buyer, artifact, expansion" at the end of the meeting. Test this.
