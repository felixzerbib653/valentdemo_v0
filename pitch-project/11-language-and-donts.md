# Language and don'ts

The words and framings that compound. The ones that backfire. This file exists because the prior product iteration (v3) drifted into language that confused buyers, and recovering took a clean-slate rebuild.

## Words that work

- **Trust grid.** The product's primary surface and a name a non-technical buyer can repeat after one demo. Use it as the noun.
- **Pillar.** The compliance dimension. Better than "control," "category," or "requirement" — pillars are sturdy, structural, plural by default, and map to the seven we ship.
- **Evidence graph.** The platform thesis in two words. Use it when investors want to understand the data asset under the workflow.
- **Audit bundle.** The artifact. Always "bundle," not "report" or "package." Bundle implies a curated set of evidence with a wrapper; report implies a static document.
- **Continuous compliance.** The category. Borrowed from Vanta deliberately. The phrase is in the air; we ride it.
- **Operator workflow.** The product positioning relative to "IT project" or "QMS." Operator is the user, workflow is the verb-shape, both signal mid-market product not enterprise install.
- **Provenance.** When describing the chain-of-custody on extracted data. Auditors and inspectors recognize the word. Investors hear it as defensibility.
- **Wedge / expansion.** Internal vocabulary that's also useful externally — every VC understands the model. Use it in the pitch when discussing the four-year arc.
- **Mid-market.** The ICP descriptor. Specific enough to filter, broad enough to scale.

## Words that backfire

- **Margin recovery.** Banned in wedge-mode pitch material. It's the v3 framing that pulled the product into a category fight (BI tools, pricing optimization) where we have no Vanta-style anchor. Margin lives in `05-expansion-arc.md` and only surfaces in the deck on the expansion slide.
- **Bidding sandbox.** Same reason. Year 2 expansion surface. Don't lead with it.
- **AI agent / autonomous agent.** Investors are saturated on agent language. We use "AI-native extraction" and "agentic surfaces" only when the context warrants. We don't pitch agent-first.
- **Platform.** Using "platform" in the wedge slide makes the product sound undisciplined. Use "product" until the expansion slide. The platform language is reserved for the expansion arc.
- **Disrupt / revolutionize / reinvent.** Standard pitch-deck inflation. Investors discount these words automatically. Use precise verbs — "absorb the 20% of QMS workflow that belongs to Compliance Ops," not "reinvent QMS."
- **Solution.** Use "product." A product has a shape; a solution is consultancy-speak.
- **Eat the world / eat the lunch.** Pattern-broken metaphors. Use literal economic claims.
- **End-to-end.** Almost always vague. Be specific about which surfaces are in scope.

## Hedge words to remove

The build-mode style guide bans **"genuinely," "honestly," "straightforward."** Same applies to pitch material. They're verbal tics that signal the speaker is hedging exactly the claim they're making. Replace with specific evidence or remove the sentence.

Also remove: "we believe," "we think," "we hope," "potentially," "could possibly," "might one day." Make the claim or don't make it.

## Framings to avoid

**"Margin-first wedge."** Confuses the buyer. Compliance is the wedge. Margin is the expansion. This was the v3 mistake.

**"We're not Vanta, but..."** If you're inviting the comparison, lean into it positively. The right move is "Vanta-shape product for an adjacent vertical and a different evidence plane" — say what's true, then explain the differences.

**"AI-first compliance for everyone."** Too broad. The frame is mid-market regulated physical manufacturing, starting with cosmetic CMOs. Specificity earns credibility.

**"$X billion TAM" without bottoms-up math.** Investors discount top-down TAM claims. Always have bottoms-up — facility count × ACV × penetration assumption — ready when asked.

**"We replace [incumbent name]."** We don't replace Veeva. We don't replace MasterControl. We sell to a buyer they don't reach with a product shape they can't ship. "Replacement" framing invites a feature-comparison fight we lose on surface area.

## The Jim test

Before any pitch, run the Jim test: would a CEO who watched the demo over Sarah's shoulder be able to repeat **wedge, buyer, artifact, expansion** by the end of the meeting?

- Wedge: MoCRA-driven supplier compliance for cosmetic CMOs.
- Buyer: Compliance Ops operator champions, CEO/COO signs.
- Artifact: the audit bundle, generated in under 60 seconds, sent to retailers.
- Expansion: bidding intelligence, then margin recovery, then category expansion.

If a slide doesn't earn its place against one of those four, cut it. Pitch slides are not status reports — they are vocabulary delivery vehicles. Every slide should plant a phrase the audience can repeat to their partners.

## On the artifact in the pitch

The most common pitch error is to describe the audit bundle instead of showing it. The bundle is the demo's most quotable moment — the "watch this" moment — and it should be triggered live, on a real (demo) supplier, with the timer running.

Generate, render, scroll, close. Three seconds of dead air while the bundle assembles is fine — it's the moment the audience sees the product do the thing the slides described. Don't talk through the assembly. Let the product carry it.
