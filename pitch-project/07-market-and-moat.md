# Market and moat

## Bottoms-up TAM — be honest about the wedge size

Wedge market, US cosmetic CMOs:

- Total cosmetic manufacturing facilities in the US: roughly 2,000 across contract and brand-owned (FDA registration data, plus industry estimates).
- Of those, contract manufacturers (third-party CMOs producing for brands): ~600.
- Mid-market ICP (2–20 facilities, $10M–$250M revenue): ~150.
- Small CMO (1–2 facility, sub-$10M): ~350. Defer to Year 2.
- Large/enterprise (20+ facility, Veeva-target): ~100. Not our buyer.

Wedge ACV: $30–80k for the mid-market band, $15–25k for the small band, dependent on facility count and supplier volume.

Wedge ARR ceiling at full mid-market penetration: roughly $4.5–12M ARR. Add the small band: $5–9M more. Total cosmetics-only US wedge: $9–21M ARR.

That's not a venture-scale market. We say so out loud in the pitch — it's the credibility move. Then we pivot to the second-vertical wedge and the expansion surface.

## Second-vertical wedge — dietary supplements

Supplements is the Year 3 vertical wedge, not a Year 4 category-expansion bullet, because the enforcement data is hot now and the failure mode is named.

- 21 CFR Part 111 observations increased 46% from 2023 to 2024 — active, accelerating FDA enforcement on supplement cGMP compliance.
- Named failure mode: supplier Certificates of Analysis accepted blindly; identity testing not performed at receipt. This is a pillar-level evidence weakness that maps directly into our framework.
- The regulatory text mandates that manufacturers qualify each vendor but provides no guidance on how to perform that qualification. A mandate without a method is a textbook market failure, and software is what fills it.

Wedge market, US dietary supplement contract manufacturers:

- Total dietary supplement manufacturers in the US: ~2,000.
- Of those, contract manufacturers: ~700.
- Mid-market ICP (2–20 facilities): ~180.
- Small (1–2 facility): ~400. Defer to Year 4.
- Large/enterprise: ~120. Not our buyer.

Wedge ACV: $25–70k for mid-market, slightly below cosmetics because retailer overlays are less codified.

Wedge ARR ceiling at full mid-market penetration: roughly $4.5–13M ARR. Combined with cosmetics: $14–34M ARR for the two-vertical wedge. Still below venture-scale on its own — the platform thesis carries the rest.

## Expansion TAM — where the venture story lives

Beyond the two wedge verticals (cosmetics + supplements), additional regulated physical supply chains, US:

- **Food additives and specialty ingredients** (FSMA 204, FDA Food Safety): ~3,000 specialty food manufacturers.
- **Nutraceuticals**: overlap with supplements but distinct regulatory surface.
- **Specialty industrial chemicals** (REACH for EU exposure, TSCA for US): ~2,500 mid-market chemical producers.
- **Veterinary products** (FDA CVM): ~400.

Conservative blended ACV across these: $40–100k. Total expansion TAM at mid-market penetration: $300–800M ARR.

Layer in international: EU CSRD and CBAM bring European manufacturers under disclosure obligations that map to the same product shape. Add another 1.5–2x.

Long-horizon TAM: $2–4B for AI-native compliance and trust infrastructure for regulated physical supply chains. Not the seed pitch's hero number, but the answer when a Series A investor asks "how big can this get."

Healthcare vendor credentialing is structurally adjacent but not a natural extension of the same product. The compliance direction is inverted (vendor manages credentials outward to many health systems, vs CMO managing supplier compliance inward), the buyer is different, and incumbents (symplr, Vendormate, GHX) hold a $1B+ existing market. We mention healthcare in pitches as long-horizon optionality, not as part of the same product roadmap.

## What we are not the TAM for

We are not the TAM for QMS (Veeva, MasterControl, ComplianceQuest are larger and serve enterprise). We are not the TAM for procurement (Coupa, Ariba, GEP). We are not the TAM for ERP supplier modules. Conflating those would inflate our market and weaken our credibility with investors who know the space. The discipline is to claim a real category — operator-grade compliance workflow with AI-native extraction — and not pretend to be QMS.

## The moat — five layers

**1. The evidence graph.** Every supplier evidence document we ingest makes the next extraction faster, the next pillar-scoring sharper, and the next bundle more complete. The graph isn't a dataset we sell — it's a learned schema for unstructured supplier evidence that compounds across the customer base. Customer N+1 onboards faster than customer N. This is the AI-native equivalent of a network effect on first-party data.

**2. Per-vertical regulatory surface knowledge.** Pillar definitions, weights, supersession rules, and program-format mappings (Sephora Clean, Ulta, etc.) are domain knowledge encoded as data. Maintaining and updating that knowledge per-vertical — quarterly, as regulations move — is real work that a generalist competitor can't replicate quickly. Vertical SaaS companies live and die on this kind of moat.

**3. Workflow and audit-history lock-in.** Once a year of audit history lives in Valent — flag resolutions, remediation notes, supplier handshakes, bundles sent and received — pulling it out is a months-long migration. Operator habit reinforces it; Sarah's daily workflow is in the product. This is classic SaaS stickiness, applied to a workflow that incumbents haven't owned.

**4. Artifact-driven distribution.** The audit bundle is the marketing surface. Every customer produces 50–200 bundles a year, each forwarded to retailer auditors, brand QA, FDA inspectors, and procurement teams. That's a compounding source of inbound leads that doesn't appear on a CAC line. Vanta proved this loop works in adjacent compliance.

**5. The Compliance Ops operator network.** Sarah and her peers move between CMOs every 3–5 years. An operator who used Valent at one CMO becomes an internal advocate at the next. We invest in the operator community early — content, events, cert program — and that investment compounds against the next generation of buying decisions.

## What is not a moat

Being an AI extraction tool is not a moat. The model layer is commoditizing fast and any competitor will hit the same 92–97% accuracy ceiling within a year. The product is the workflow, the bundle, and the graph. We say "AI-native" to investors as a credibility marker for our cost structure and time-to-value, not as a defensibility claim.
