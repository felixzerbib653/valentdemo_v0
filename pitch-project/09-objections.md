# Objections and responses

The ten objections that show up in seed-stage conversations. Each one with a strong answer, not a hedge. Practice these out loud before the meeting.

## 1. "The market is too narrow."

The wedge market is narrow by design. US cosmetic CMOs at the mid-market band cap at roughly $9–21M ARR. We say so out loud — it's a credibility move and it pre-empts the objection. The reason we're worth funding is the expansion surface: dietary supplements, food specialty ingredients, nutraceuticals, specialty industrial chemicals. Same product shape, modular pillar definitions per vertical, same buyer archetype. Expansion TAM is $2–4B on a decade horizon. The wedge proves the product, the expansion proves the platform.

If the investor pushes back with "every founder says they'll expand," the answer is: we built the product around the evidence graph from day one, not as a per-vertical app. The pillar framework is configurable data, not custom code. Show them the schema in the demo data — it's already vertical-agnostic underneath.

## 2. "Vanta will eat this."

Vanta's sales motion is self-serve to mid-market SaaS, sold to IT/Security. That motion does not translate to regulated physical manufacturing where the buyer is Compliance Ops, the evidence plane is unstructured, and the first facility requires white-glove onboarding. Vanta would have to build a different sales org, retrain a different go-to-market, and absorb a different cost structure to compete in our market. They will eventually — five years out — and at that point we want to be the acquisition target, not the casualty.

In the meantime, the analogy works for us, not against us. Every Vanta brand impression makes our positioning easier. We benefit from their category creation.

## 3. "Why won't Veeva or MasterControl roll this out?"

Veeva Vault and MasterControl are enterprise QMS platforms with $200k+ entry pricing and 6–18 month implementation cycles. Mid-market CMOs ($10–250M revenue) cannot absorb that cost or that timeline. The QMS vendors' channel partners optimize for $500k+ deals. Their cost structure makes mid-market unprofitable at our price point.

For them to enter, they'd need a separate product line, a separate sales motion, and a separate partner channel. Plausible at the 3–5 year horizon. By then we own the evidence graph and the operator habit, and the better outcome for them is to acquire us than to build it.

## 4. "Compliance is a checkbox. It's not a business."

Tell that to the CMO that just paid a $180k chargeback because their supplier's FEI registration lapsed and the retailer's QA team caught it. Tell that to the operator who lost a Sephora bid because she couldn't produce the supplier evidence in the 5-day window. Compliance is a checkbox until it isn't, and the moments when it isn't are catastrophic line items.

The deeper answer: continuous compliance is not "checkbox compliance." Vanta proved the category by transforming SOC 2 from an annual audit ritual into a monthly evidence cadence, and that transformation created a $2B-revenue company. We're doing the same transformation in physical supply chains.

## 5. "What's actually defensible? AI extraction is commoditizing."

We agree. The AI extraction is necessary but not sufficient. The defensibility lives in five layers: the evidence graph (compounding first-party data and learned schema), per-vertical regulatory surface knowledge (real domain investment), workflow and audit-history lock-in (operator habit), artifact-driven distribution (the bundle as marketing surface), and the operator network that moves between CMOs and brings us with them.

A competitor with a better LLM and a better demo can match the extraction in six months. They cannot replicate two years of customer audit history, three years of vertical pillar definitions, or the daily workflow embedded in the operator's morning.

## 6. "Regulatory risk — what if MoCRA gets rolled back?"

MoCRA could be weakened in some specific provision, but the underlying compliance trend is multi-decade and bipartisan. FSMA, DSCSA, REACH, EU CSRD — none of these go backwards. The retailer-driven overlays (Sephora Clean, Ulta) are private compliance regimes that don't depend on FDA enforcement at all.

More importantly, the product's wedge is MoCRA but the spine is the evidence graph. If MoCRA were repealed tomorrow, we'd lose the urgency of the sales motion but not the underlying product value — supplier evidence still needs to be tracked for retailer audits, brand QA, and FDA Form 482 inspections.

## 7. "Why is the sales cycle short enough to be a SaaS business?"

It is — 3–6 months from first conversation to paid pilot, 6–9 months to commercial deal. The accelerator is the operator champion. Sarah-archetype Compliance Ops operators experience the pain daily and become internal advocates within 30 minutes of a demo. The economic buyer (CEO/COO) signs because the operator and the avoided-chargeback math close the case.

We are not selling enterprise software with a 12-month evaluation cycle. We are selling an operator workflow tool with a price point that fits within a department head's discretionary budget. The sales motion is closer to Vanta's than Veeva's.

## 8. "Are you SaaS or services?"

SaaS, with white-glove onboarding for the first one or two facilities of a new customer. Onboarding is two weeks, billed as a one-time implementation fee or rolled into the first year. After year one, expansion (additional facilities, additional pillars, program-format add-ons) is self-serve through the product.

Gross margin target year one: 65–70% blended (pulled down by services). Year three: 78–82% as the customer base shifts to expansion revenue. Standard vertical SaaS shape.

## 9. "How do you compete with in-house tools?"

The in-house tool — SharePoint plus Excel plus a shared inbox — is what every prospect already runs. That's actually our advantage. The competitor is inertia, not a vendor. Every demo begins with "show us your current process" and the gap becomes obvious in five minutes. We don't have to displace a competitor's product; we have to displace a process that the operator already hates.

The conversion math is favorable: operators are the champion, the pain is acute, and the ROI shows up in the first month. The losses we incur are slow-deciding CEOs, not lost demos.

## 10. "What does this look like in 18 months?"

20 paying mid-market cosmetic CMO customers, $1.5–2.5M ARR, 110%+ net revenue retention from facility expansions and program-format add-ons, the first dietary-supplement design partner signed, and a bid-intelligence module in the product roadmap with one customer in beta. That's the Series A story.

If the investor wants the more aggressive shape: 30 customers, $3M ARR, two verticals live, the first inbound from a Sephora-adjacent retailer asking for a vendor-side program tool. That's the upside case if the artifact-driven distribution lights up faster than the base plan.

## 11. "Why not start with supplements? The enforcement looks hotter there."

It does. 21 CFR Part 111 observations rose 46% from 2023 to 2024, the named failure mode (CoAs accepted blindly, identity testing skipped) is exactly the kind of evidence weakness our pillar framework solves, and the regulatory text mandates vendor qualification without prescribing a method — which is the textbook market-failure setup for software.

We considered it. Cosmetics first is the right call for three reasons that don't show up in pure enforcement data.

First, calendar-event regulation sells software faster than steady-state enforcement. MoCRA has named deadlines — facility registration, product listing, safety substantiation — that create a "must close by" moment for the buyer. 21 CFR Part 111 is steady-state enforcement; observations are accelerating but there's no specific date that puts a CFO's foot on the accelerator. Calendar events shorten sales cycles. Steady-state pain shortens close rates without shortening cycles.

Second, design-partner access is real today in cosmetics and hypothetical in supplements. Elevation Labs is a confirmed design partner. Switching to a supplements-first wedge means starting design-partner cultivation from scratch — losing 6–9 months of validation runway to chase a market with marginally better enforcement signal. The wedge sequencing is partly a function of where the founder already has access, not just where the macro looks best.

Third, retailer-overlay programs are more codified in cosmetics. Sephora Clean, Ulta Conscious Beauty, Credo Clean Standard, and Target Clean are named programs with documented requirements that compound onto MoCRA enforcement and create a second buying signal. Supplements has retailer overlays — Whole Foods Premium, GNC, Amazon's supplement compliance push — but they're less codified and don't carry the same brand resonance with investors. The Sephora Clean line in the pitch is rhetorical lift the supplements pitch doesn't get.

The synthesis: cosmetics first, supplements parallel-tracked starting Year 2 with paid GA in Year 3. We don't defer supplements to Year 4 — the enforcement data is real and the buying urgency is building. We just don't lead with it.
