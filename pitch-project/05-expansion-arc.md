# Expansion arc — wedge to platform

## The four-year shape

**Year 1 — Compliance, cosmetics (the wedge).** Seven pillars, MoCRA, audit bundles, 12–20 mid-market cosmetic CMOs as design partners converting to paid. ACV $30–80k. The product earns its seat on the compliance spend line in the operator budget. This is what the current demo shows.

**Year 2 — Bidding intelligence + supplements design-partner cultivation.** Two parallel motions.

The cross-sell motion: the continuous trust data we already have per supplier becomes deal intelligence. When a cosmetics CMO bids on a new brand program, they need to show compliance posture across the ingredient list. Valent already has that, already structured, already ranked. The bid workspace is a new surface that pulls from the existing evidence graph and produces program-qualification artifacts (Sephora Clean, Ulta Conscious Beauty, Target Clean, Credo Clean Standard). ACV lift: 1.5–2x on the existing customer base.

The second-vertical motion: begin design-partner cultivation in dietary supplements. 21 CFR Part 111 observations increased 46% from 2023 to 2024 — active, accelerating enforcement, with a named failure mode (supplier CoAs accepted blindly, identity testing not performed). The regulatory text mandates vendor qualification but provides no method, which is a textbook market failure. The pillar framework maps directly: FDA registration, cGMP SOPs, identity testing, supplier qualification, adverse event reporting, label compliance, origin and chain-of-custody. No paid supplements revenue in Year 2 — but 3–5 design partners locked in, pillar framework validated, and the go-to-market motion rehearsed.

**Year 3 — Supplements wedge ships + margin/substitution in cosmetics.** Two product ships.

Supplements wedge goes GA: paid supplements CMOs onboarding, target 8–12 paying customers by year-end, ACV $25–70k. Retailer overlays for supplements (Whole Foods Premium, GNC, Amazon supplement policy, Sprouts) become program-format variants of the bundle, mirroring the cosmetic retailer-overlay pattern. Entry into supplements at Year 3 instead of Year 4 is an explicit acceleration driven by the enforcement data; deferring it further leaves 18–30 months of buying urgency on the table.

Margin and substitution layer goes beta in cosmetics: per-ingredient cost and substitution data on top of the evidence graph. An operator (now likely procurement, not compliance) can see: "ingredient X is compliant but sourced from one supplier at $Y; substitute W is compliant, passes the same pillar weights, and costs $Y–15%." This is the margin recovery story the v3 product tried to lead with — but it only works as the third layer of a product whose first two layers earn the right to have cost data. ACV lift: another 1.5–2x on the cosmetics customer base, new buyer (COO or procurement leadership).

**Year 4 — Margin GA + international + adjacent-vertical optionality.** Margin/substitution ships GA in cosmetics and beta in supplements. International expansion begins (EU CPR, Health Canada, UK OPSS) by layering region-specific pillars onto existing customers who export. Adjacent regulated-physical verticals (food additives under FSMA 204, specialty industrial chemicals under REACH/TSCA, nutraceuticals, veterinary products) are scoped but not necessarily shipped — the third and fourth verticals are Series B conversations, not seed commitments.

Healthcare vendor credentialing is a long-horizon optionality, not an extension of the same product. It's an inverted compliance problem (vendor managing credentials outward to many health systems, vs CMO managing supplier compliance inward) and competes against established incumbents (symplr, Vendormate, GHX). Named here so investors who know the market hear that we know the difference. Not a Year 4 ship.

## Why this arc and not a different one

The arc respects three constraints.

**Buyer continuity.** Year 1 buyer is Compliance Ops, signed off by CEO/COO. Year 2 keeps Compliance Ops as primary user but pulls in Procurement (bidding) and extends to a new vertical with the same buyer archetype (supplements). Year 3 pulls Procurement and Finance on margin. Year 4 deepens existing buyers and begins international. At no point do we ask the existing customer to buy a new product for a new buyer. Each layer extends the footprint with the people who already know us.

**Data reuse.** Year 2's bid intelligence uses Year 1's evidence graph with no new ingestion. Year 3's substitution sandbox uses Year 1's pillar scoring with cost data layered in. Supplements reuses the product surface with new pillar definitions — same schema, new weights, new retailer overlays. Every dollar we spend building the wedge compounds into the expansion. We don't have five products; we have one product with four rings and two verticals.

**Wedge sequencing discipline.** Cosmetics stays the wedge even though supplements has stronger current enforcement data. Calendar-event regulation (MoCRA) sells software faster than steady-state enforcement (21 CFR 111). Design-partner access (Elevation Labs) is real today in cosmetics and hypothetical in supplements. Retailer-overlay programs are more codified in cosmetics. The wedge decision is a function of more than enforcement urgency — see `09-objections.md` #11 for the full case.

## Why we don't pitch this as one slide in the seed round

The expansion arc is the reason VCs fund the wedge, but it's also the place where a founder can lose the audience by overselling. Our pitch discipline is: wedge slide up front, expansion slide near the end, and a clear gate between them — "we're selling Year 1 now; we've proven to ourselves the ladder to Years 2–4; investors who want a thesis on that layer should ask." Make them lean in.

A previous iteration of the product put margin recovery on slide two. It backfired because margin is a lagging output of the evidence graph, and selling it first made the wedge look like a Trojan horse. The wedge has to be a real product that a real operator will pay for on its own merits. The expansion is the upside case, not the base case.

## What the Jim-archetype CEO (Alpine-PE audience) needs to hear

This audience cares about two things in the expansion arc. First, does the product land in Year 1 without requiring customization per customer — because PE-backed rollup economics need product-led margin. Second, does the expansion land in the same buyer budget — because cross-buyer selling extends payback periods. Our answer to both is yes: the pillar framework is configurable data, not custom code; and the expansion stays inside the compliance/procurement/operations triangle.

When Jim asks "what's Year 3" in a pitch, the answer is "supplements wedge ships and per-ingredient margin recovery in cosmetics goes beta — we get there by first owning compliance evidence in cosmetics for two years." He should leave the pitch able to repeat "wedge, buyer, artifact, expansion" from memory.
