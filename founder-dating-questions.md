# Founder dating — questions before we both quit

## How to use this

Founder dating isn't a personality quiz. It's a stress test for the conversations you'll wish you'd had 18 months in, when things are hard. Each section below is calibrated to a specific way co-founder partnerships rupture — money, equity drift, divergent exit timelines, silent conflict, life pressure outside the company.

Two rules:

1. Answer these alone, in writing, **before** the conversation. If you haven't thought about it yourself, his answer will anchor yours.
2. Questions that make you want to change the subject are the ones to sit with. The awkwardness is the signal.

Budget a full day, not a dinner. Trade written answers out loud. Write down what you actually agreed on at the end and date it. Revisit in 90 days.

If more than three answers surprise you — on either side — slow down. A bad co-founder fit is the most expensive mistake in early-stage, and it's almost always visible in month zero if you look.

---

## 1. Why us, why this

The goal here is to separate "I'm excited about working with you" from "I'm convicted about this problem." You need both. Only one and the partnership bends under stress.

- Why are you working on this specific problem, and would you still work on it if I dropped out tomorrow?
- What does "this worked" look like 5 years from now — revenue shape, team size, your day-to-day?
- If we raised $5M next quarter but had to pivot off the current thesis, would you still be in? What's the version of this you'd walk away from?
- What else did you seriously consider building, and why this over that?

## 2. Money and runway

This is the section most founders underinvest in, and it's the most predictive. Asymmetric personal finances quietly deform every other decision — salary timing, fundraising pace, willingness to hold during a slow quarter.

- How many months of personal runway do you have before your life materially changes? Rent/mortgage, kids' tuition, health insurance dependents, aging parents.
- What salary do you need from the company to be sustainable — not ideal, but sustainable — and when do you need it by?
- If we raise a small seed, are you willing to stay at $0–$60k total comp for another 12 months after close?
- If one of us can take a lower salary than the other and it's structurally unfair, how do we handle it?
- Any outstanding debts, obligations, visa status, or side income that affects your focus or ownership calculus? (Ask plainly. This isn't rude — it's the conversation.)

## 3. Equity, roles, and decision rights

Most co-founder equity fights aren't about the split at formation. They're about the drift. One partner ends up doing 70% of the work or raising the money alone, and the 50/50 starts to feel like a tax.

- Should the split be 50/50, and why? Where do you think the asymmetry in contribution will actually show up?
- Who has the final word on: product roadmap, engineering architecture, hiring, fundraising terms, board composition, firing decisions?
- What title do you want, and what happens if an investor pushes back on it during diligence?
- What vesting schedule feels right to you? Cliff, acceleration, reverse-vesting for existing IP — do you have strong priors?
- If I'm CEO and you think I'm making a bad commercial call, what's your move? What happens if we don't converge?
- If you're CTO and I think you're over-engineering, same question back.

## 4. Work style and intensity

The answers here matter less than the honesty. If your partner tells you he'll do 70-hour weeks and he's never actually done one, that's a data point. If he says 45 and means it, that might be the better signal.

- What's a sustainable week for you — hours, weekends, late nights — for the next 12 months?
- What are your non-negotiable blocks (kids, partner, therapy, workouts, faith practice, sleep) and when?
- How do you prefer to work async vs. sync? Where do most co-founder friction points come from, in your experience?
- What's your worst professional habit? (The one your last manager would name first.)

## 5. Conflict, trust, feedback

This is the hardest section to answer usefully in the abstract. Look for specificity in his stories. Vague answers here predict silent resentment later.

- Tell me about a time you had a real, sustained disagreement with a peer at work. How did it resolve, and what would you do differently?
- When you're frustrated with a coworker, what's your default — confront, withdraw, go around, escalate?
- What kind of feedback is hard for you to hear? What kind lands well?
- If I noticed you missing commitments for three weeks straight, how do you want me to raise it? And if you noticed it in me?
- Have we ever disagreed on something that mattered? If not, what does that tell us?

## 6. The business shape — fundraising, exits, team

A surprising number of co-founders only discover in year 2 that one of them was building for a $30M acquihire and the other was building for an IPO. Surface the outcome shape now.

- On a scale from lifestyle business to venture-backed billion-dollar outcome, where do you want this to land, and why?
- Would you accept an acquisition offer at 2–3x the seed round, if investors wanted an early exit?
- How many years are you willing to give this before you'd consider it "not working"?
- Who do we hire first — and why? Engineer, designer, GTM, ops?
- What's your hiring bar? What do you refuse to tolerate in a team member?
- What's your relationship to fundraising — energizing, draining, somewhere in between? How much of it do you want to own?

## 7. Life outside the company

Founders underestimate how much the partner, kids, health, and housing situation affect runway tolerance and risk appetite. The company and the rest of life are one system.

- Does your spouse / partner know you're doing this, and are they genuinely aligned on the risk — financially and otherwise?
- Any life events planned in the next 2 years that affect commitment — kids, relocation, health, family obligations?
- What do you do when you burn out? How will I know you're burning out before you do?
- What's the life you're trying to build with this company — the material piece and the non-material piece?

## 8. The dissolution scenario

The hardest section to broach, and the one that protects both of you most. Agree on the contingency while you still like each other.

- If in month 9 we decide it isn't working — business or partnership — what does a clean exit look like for each of us?
- If one of us quits, what happens to the shares? What's the buyback mechanic, at what valuation, over what period?
- What would you need to see me do — or stop doing — for you to leave?
- What would I need to see you do — or stop doing — for me to ask you to leave? How do we have that conversation?

---

## Before the meeting — answer these yourself first

Treat this as the real prep, not just the in-meeting flow.

- Write down your honest answers to every question above. In sentences, not bullets.
- Name your top three dealbreakers. The ones where a wrong answer ends the conversation.
- Name the two questions you're most afraid to ask. Ask those first.
- Prep one concrete story from your own work history for each of §4 and §5. Specifics beat philosophy.

## After the meeting — the one-pager

Before you shake hands and quit your jobs, write a shared one-pager covering:

- Split, vesting, decision-rights map.
- Salary floors and when each of you starts drawing.
- Runway tolerance — months of $0 you're each prepared for.
- Exit outcome range you're both building toward.
- How we handle disagreement on the five decisions in §3.
- What triggers a "this isn't working" conversation, and when.

Sign it. Date it. Revisit in 90 days, then every 6 months.

---

*The questions you don't ask become the fights you can't avoid. Better to have the awkward morning than the expensive year.*
